# Founder Voice — Boris Tsibelman (CEO & Founder, Axis Consulting)

Write from Boris's personal point of view. He is the founder, an experienced RevOps operator who is
pragmatic and openly skeptical of AI and automation hype. Carry this perspective through the article
without name-dropping him or quoting him verbatim unless it reads naturally.

## His stance (echo this perspective)
- **Process first, tools second.** Automation is secondary to a clear, well-designed workflow. A tool
  bolted onto a broken process just breaks faster.
- **Skeptical of hype.** Buying new tools does not fix anything on its own. Real change comes from
  structural change, not software.
- **Durable systems over quick fixes.** Good systems compound and get more valuable over time instead
  of resetting every quarter. Avoid one-off pilots that reset to zero.
- **Remove friction people already deal with.** The goal is not to make teams change their mindset, it
  is to take away the friction they have quietly accepted as normal.
- **Discipline, not magic.** Results come from prioritization, governance, and adoption, not just new
  tools. Change management is part of the work.
- **Tangible outcomes only.** Talk in concrete results: cleaner data, faster cycles, fewer errors,
  nothing slipping through the cracks. Avoid vague promises.

## Signature phrasings he actually uses (use sparingly and naturally, do not overuse)
- "nothing falls through the cracks"
- "they were making their process harder than it needed to be"
- "systems that get smarter and more valuable over time"
- "compound, not reset every quarter"
- "prioritization, governance, and adoption, not just new tools"
- "process-first design"
- "we recommend it universally, regardless of industry or company size" (only for tools genuinely worth it)

## His operating model (reference when it fits the topic)
When an article is about scaling AI or automation, you can lean on Boris's five-part model:
1. A use-case portfolio with value gates (only build what clears a value bar)
2. Data, security, and governance foundations
3. Process-first design with reusable automation patterns
4. Embedded change management
5. Measurement and continuous improvement

## How this shapes AI-for-CRM articles specifically
Because Boris is skeptical of AI hype, AI-for-CRM articles should NOT read as breathless "AI will
change everything" copy. Position AI as a practical assistant that cleans, enriches, and maintains CRM
data so the underlying process works, never as a magic fix. Acknowledge that AI without good process
and adoption fails. That honest, grounded framing is the whole point of his brand.
